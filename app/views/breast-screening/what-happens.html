Breast screening (mammogram)

What happens at your appointment - Breast screening (mammogram)

During breast screening you'll have 2 breast X-rays (mammograms) done on each breast.

The mammograms are done by a female health specialist (mammographer). The mammograms should take around 15 minutes. The whole appointment should take about 30 minutes.

Before starting, the mammographer will check your details with you and ask if you have had any breast problems.

They will also explain what will happen during the screening and answer any questions you have.

Things to tell the screening team when you book your appointment

To help the team plan for your screening properly and make any necessary arrangements, tell them if you:

  • have a disability
  • have problems with your mobility – wheelchair access is available at most centres and some mobile units
  • have breast implants – you may need an extra X-ray to get a clearer picture around the implant
  • are pregnant, think you could be pregnant or are breastfeeding
  • are under the care of a breast consultant
  • have had a mammogram in the last 6 months
  • need an interpreter if your first language is not English
  • have had a coronavirus vaccine within the past 5 days – some people may have swollen glands in their armpit which can be picked up by the X-rays

How breast screening is done

Breast screening is usually done by 1 or 2 mammographers. You can ask them about any questions or concerns you have.

  1. You’ll need to undress, in a changing area, so you are naked to the waist. You may be given a hospital gown to put on.
  2. You’ll be called into the X-ray room and the mammographer will explain what will happen.
  3. The mammographer will place your breast onto the X-ray machine. It will be flattened between 2 pieces of plastic to keep it still while the X-rays are taken. This takes a few seconds.
  4. The X-ray machine will then be tilted to one side and the process will be repeated on the side of your breast.
  5. Your other breast will be X-rayed in the same way.
  6. You will then return to the changing area to get dressed.

Important: Important

Breast screening is often uncomfortable and sometimes painful for some people.

You can talk to the mammographer, who is trained to help you feel more comfortable and give you support. You can also ask to stop at any time.

Breast screening if you are trans or non-binary

If you have not had top surgery (chest reconstruction) and wear a binder, you will need to remove this before having a mammogram.

Private changing areas are available, so you can remove your binder just before the mammogram.

If you have any worries or questions you can:

  • talk to your local breast screening service - find breast screening services
  • contact the Macmillan Cancer Support support line
  • bring someone with you to your appointment

You can find out more about when you'll be invited and who should go for breast screening.

Things to help your breast screening appointment

You do not need to do anything special to prepare for a mammogram. But there are things that may help.

Don't

  • do not use talcum powder or spray deodorant on the day as this may affect the mammogram. Roll-on deodorant is ok.

Do

  • wear a skirt or trousers, rather than a dress, to make it easier to get naked to the waist
    • remove necklaces and large earrings before you arrive for your appointment tell them if you have found screening uncomfortable in the past
  • talk to the staff if you are nervous or embarrassed, they are trained to help you feel more comfortable and provide support
  • if there are any phrases or words that make you uncomfortable or nervous, ask the staff not to use them
  • if you would like to, tell the staff your pronouns

Things to look out for after breast screening

Any physical discomfort or pain you may have during a mammogram should go away very soon.

If you found the mammogram very painful you may have pain for a couple of days. This will go away but see a GP if it does not.
