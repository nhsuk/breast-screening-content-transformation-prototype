Breast screening (mammogram)

When you'll be invited and who should go - Breast screening (mammogram)

Anyone registered with a GP as a woman will be invited for NHS breast screening every 3 years between the ages of 50 and 71. You will get a letter in the post inviting you.

When you'll be invited

You will automatically be invited for your first breast screening between the ages of 50 and 53. Then every 3 years until your 71st birthday.

You need to be registered with a GP surgery to be invited for breast screening.

Read more about how to register with a GP.

Information:

GOV.UK have an easy guide to breast screening for people with a learning disability to help you understand breast screening and decide if you want to go.

If you have not been sent a letter

If you have not been invited for breast screening by the time you are 53 and think you should have been, contact your local breast screening service.

If you're over 71

You will not automatically be invited for breast screening if you are over 71.

But you can still have breast screening every 3 years if you want to. You will need to call your local breast screening service.

Find breast screening services

If you're trans or non-binary

If you are a trans man, trans woman or are non-binary you’ll:

  • automatically be invited for breast screening if you’re registered as female with a GP
  • not automatically be invited for breast screening if you’re registered as male with a GP
What to do if you were assigned female at birth

If you've not had surgery to remove your breasts and male chest reconstruction (top surgery), you should have breast screening.

If you've had top surgery, you may still have some breast tissue. But it’s unlikely you will be able to have a mammogram. Talk to a GP if you notice any changes in your chest tissue.

If you think you should have breast screening, but you are not invited automatically, talk to your GP surgery or call your local breast screening service to book.

Find breast screening services
What to do if you were assigned male at birth

If you’ve been taking feminising hormones for longer than 2 years, you should have breast screening. These hormones can increase your chance of getting breast cancer.

If you think you should have breast screening, but you are not invited automatically, talk to your GP surgery or call your local breast screening service to book.

Find breast screening services
Information:

Cancer Research UK have more information on cancer screening if you are trans or non-binary.

If you have a higher chance of getting breast cancer

Some people are more likely to get breast cancer. This is sometimes called moderate risk or high risk.

You may have a higher chance of getting breast cancer if you have:

  • several close relatives who have had breast or ovarian cancer or both
  • you have a change in a gene (mutation) that makes you more likely to get breast cancer – these can include BRCA1, BRCA2 or TP53
What to do if you think you have a higher chance of getting breast cancer

Speak to a GP if you think you might have a higher chance of getting breast cancer.

They can refer you to a genetic specialist who will talk to you about your risk. You may need to have genetic tests.

Find out more about predictive genetic tests for cancer risk genes.

If you have a higher chance of getting breast cancer you may:

  • be invited for breast screening before you are 50
  • be invited more often than every 3 years
  • have MRI scans as well as mammograms - MRI can give a clearer picture in younger people

The specialist looking after you will explain how you will be screened.

Cancer Research UK have information on family history of breast cancer and inherited genes.

Find out more

  • the NHS website has information about predictive genetic tests for cancer risk genes
  • Cancer Research UK have information on family history of breast cancer and inherited genes

If you have symptoms

See a GP if you have any symptoms of breast cancer. Even if you have recently had a clear breast screening.

Do not wait for your next breast screening appointment.
